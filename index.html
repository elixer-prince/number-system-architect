Phase 1: The Blueprint

Base-10 (Decimal)

The system humans use. It has 10 digits (0-9). Every time you hit 10, you roll over to a new column (Ones, Tens, Hundreds).

Base-2 (Binary)

The system computers use. It has only 2 digits (0 and 1). Every time you hit 2, you roll over. Columns are powers of 2 (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...).

What is a Bit?

Short for BInary digiT. It is the smallest unit of data in computing, representing a single 0 or 1.

What's happening here?

In binary, each position represents a Power of 2. Starting from the right, the values are 1, 2, 4, 8, and so on. When a switch is "1" (On), its value is added to the total. When it's "0" (Off), it contributes zero. By combining these specific "weights," we can represent any whole number!

Challenge: Set switches to make exactly 23

Decimal to Binary

There are 2 main methods to perform this conversion.

1

Division Method

Repeatedly divide your number by 2 and track the remainders. Perfect for large numbers where you don't know the bit values.

2

Positional Method

Compare your number against a table of power-of-2 values. Faster for mental math and small numbers like 0-255.

Phase 3: The Division Method

Method #1
1

Divide & Track

Divide your decimal number by 2. You will get a Quotient (the result) and a Remainder (which will always be either 0 or 1).

2

Repeat Until Zero

Take the Quotient from the previous step and divide it by 2 again. Keep repeating this process until your Quotient reaches exactly 0.

3

Reverse & Read

Collect all the Remainders you found. Your binary number is the list of remainders read in reverse order (from the bottom up).
